--- title: 900$ A Stick subtitle: RAM Prices Are A Feature, Not A Bug created: 2026-04-17 edited: 2026-04-17 tags: ['AI', 'Capitalism'] ---

As of writing, the globe is in a very weird situation where more computing hardware, especially RAM, has been purchased than has actually been manufactured. This is mostly by proposed AI data centers. Bird-site user Jatin K Malik (@jatinkrmalik) may have said it best:

The reason why RAM has become four times more expensive is that a huge amount of RAM that has not yet been produced was purchased with non-existent money to be installed in GPUs that also have not yet been produced, in order to place them in data centers that have not yet been built, powered by infrastructure that may never appear, to satisfy demand that does not actually exist and to obtain profit that is mathematically impossible.

As an open and notorious computer owner and user, this is undeniably bad because the prices for everything from GPUs to raspberry pi's are through the roof.

But who is this good for? Generally, the answer provided is one that appeals to a bigger economic picture. AI companies benefit from these moves because it allows them to make financial commitments that signal to shareholders that they're expecting to continue to grow and expand, thereby increasing revenue, market penetration, and overall corporate growth. However, I think that this bigger-picture perspective, while certainly valid and accurate, misses another dynamic as well, which is that the lack of computer parts for the common consumer is actually beneficial for these companies in itself.

To anyone who has been paying attention, we are in a rent-seeking plague. If you want a movie, can you purchase it? No, you have to get it through a streaming platform like Netflix who can decide to drop it from their catalogue without warning. If you want an ebook, can you purchase it? No, you have to fake-purchase it from Amazon, which is actually selling you a perpetual license, which is a license that isn't actually perpetual. If you want to edit media can you purchase an install and own a local copy of the software? No, you have to set up a subscription to a creative cloud that keeps your projects hostage unless you keep paying and they'll also try to get you to agree to let them train AI on your data.

It used to be that selling shovels during a gold rush was the best way to make money. Now it's seeming like renting them out for a monthly price is actually far more lucrative.

The same thing has happened to computing generally. AWS is, by most accounts, the most profitable part of Amazon. What they do is say to customers that they don't have to maintain their own webservers anymore. They can simply rent them from AWS. Same with Google Cloud, Azure, and other web service providers. For most companies, this is pretty advantageous. It's worth it to them financially to simply pay a price to not have to maintain a whole stack of tech liabilities. That's also why things like microservices exist. Could an engineer build them in-house? Often yes, but it's worth it to the company to offload that responsibility to another organization in exchange for money.

But what about non-business consumers, aka regular people? This model makes less sense, although in some cases it can be justified. With the same budget, I would rather own fewer books and movies that I love and cherish than have a brief interaction with a wider variety of media, but those are my personal values. I would rather use software that lacks the latest features and updates if I can quantify it as a one-time cost rather than an ongoing subscription, but those are my personal values. Reasonable people can genuinely disagree on all these things, and I think that that's perfectly fine.

But what about computing hardware? It used to be relatively siloed from the rent plague because the computing demand of most users wasn't actually that big. Sure, there were always creatives who needed powerful rendering capabilities and gamers who needed ray-tracing and framerates, but most people just used email, word processing, spreadsheets, and other non-compute-heavy productivity tools.

This all changed with AI. The promise was always that these tools would transform work by making you more productive. It is dubious as to whether this claim is even true to begin with, and it's also clear that if there is an increase in productivity, the benefits of that are captured by the capital-owning class instead of distributed to workers. But that aside, this was a brand new opportunity where an entire class of professionals were suddenly told that they need to rely on computing capacity that isn't realistic for them to own.

AI companies knew this and started expanding computing capacities in data center constructions throughout the country. And when they did, the rhetoric around constructing this often justified it as "infrastructure", and that was very curious to me, because think about the infrastructure we already have- water, power, internet. All of these are centralized by a station, plant, or regional network, then delivered to your home. That's when it dawned on me that data center owners want to deliver compute to me the way the power station delivers power.

I have compute ability like how some people might have gas generators for various cases, but they want most of the compute I use, like most of the power I use, to come from a centralized source. Why? It's the rent plague. Step 1: Make people dependent on a technology that requires compute beyond capability they can reasonably own, then Step 2: Rent them that computing capacity. It's renting shovels in a gold mine.

There are serval problems with this. The biggest is that for most non-business users, this arrangement sucks. Currently it sucks less, because the cost of these tools is subsidized by venture capital, but it will really suck when the bubble pops and that is no longer the case.

The second problem with this, related to the first, is that the math doesn't really work out all that well. The thing about large-scale power delivery, water treatment, internet infrastructure, etc. is that is vastly cheaper to do these on a large scale then for every home to try to do it on their own. How true is this for AI? Well, it's true to a certain extent. A data center can certainly more efficiently do the same amount of computation than I can at home for a lower price. But remember, that the game here is not to make things better for people; we're in rent plague. So on one hand, the margin has to be thick enough for companies to be cheap enough to be worth it, but expensive enough for them to profit.

But that hasn't been the strategy that these companies have implemented. Instead, the move ahs been to try to increase the compute consumption of their consumers. Yesterday's model is bad. Use today's instead. It does thinking/agents/mcps/reasoning or whatever other buzzword paradigm is floating around today. The base LLM paradigm was that a user prompt gets tokenized and fed to the input layer of a neural net with instructions. The net did a forward pass, and returned the instructions to the chat window. Today they make what seem like a thousand calls to different models or parts of models, burning thousands of tokens, returning output that promises to be so much better and so much more usable. Is it though?

Even if the answer is yes, and I think it sometimes can be, this only serves to reinforce the fact that they want to rent you the shovels. Whether the shovels work or not, they want to rent them to you.

And that's where the third sucky thing comes in. Because there is a finite amount of RAM in the world, which means that ownership of that RAM is a zero sum game. So as a data center builder, you can 1. signal to shareholders you're hot stuff, 2. build compute capacity that you're telling people to rely on, and 3. you claiming capacity has the knock-on effect of raising the price of the market supply of computing hardware so high it prices most consumers out of being able to create their own capacity to compute for themselves? Jackpot!

We are in the rent plague, and the fact that the price of computer parts is sky high is very much a feature, not a bug.
