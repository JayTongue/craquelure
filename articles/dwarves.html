--- title: In Defense of Dwarves subtitle: Building Bildungsroman created: 2026-03-02 edited: 2026-03-02 tags: ['AI', 'Productivity'] ---

AI has the impact of allowing people who otherwise wouldn't be able to interact with, develop, and assemble software the ability to do so. People from all different backgrounds who don't have the experience or time to invest in a traditional computing skillset can quickly ask Codex or Claude Code to not just solve a problem but to actually build whole projects by themselves. On one hand, there are definitely upsides to this. I have relied on these tools many times to help me with tasks that I'm unfamiliar with. For instance, I recently had to expand the amount of swap on my laptop, and while I likely could have looked through man pages, Linux community forums, and stack overflow to configure this myself, I was able to do it in just a few minutes by popping my hardware and OS setup into Claude.

Some deride this as a shortcut, and that "true knowledge" lies in the friction and struggle. I will admit that I have sometimes been that person, but in that moment, it is not a priority for me to learn how to do a task that I'd likely only have to perform once. Because you bet your dingus that if I ever had to do it again, I'd go straight to AI.

This situation seems analogous to another area of interest for me, microcontrollers. They're fascinating because although they compute (in that they can take inputs and perform calculations to yield an output), they are not computers (at least in the Turing-complete universal von Neumann machine sense). This means, among other things, that you can program them, but you have to play by their rules. While you could get super low level and write assembly, or more likely C, but even going through the C process to preprocessor -> compiler -> assembly -> linker -> locator process with C code is much more laborious than just using Arduino IDE or MicroPython/CircuitPython. And for most people, like figuring out how to reallocate their own swap, there simply isn't enough benefit to doing so to justify going through all of that.

Maybe if you wanted to run a bank of ESP32's to get the maximally performant nerdminers out there, it would make sense, or if you're doing some kind of specialized drone controller that needs to have as closed to no latency as possible, then it would make sense, but for most people, it simply doesn't matter enough to justify going through the trouble of writing lower level code. The result is that I often think of these microcontrollers and the associated transducers that you can attach to them as "programable Legos". I solder things together according to what pins need to go where, then I designate the pins in Arduino IDE, and import precompiled libraries that use pre-existing protocols. There's no reason to need to re-create a protocol like SPI or I2C. Nor is there a need to re-create something like a DHT22 library. Just import it, and I'm ready to go.

I trade off deep, lower-level knowledge for the ability to build with as little friction as possible. Besides the productivity increase, there are other benefits as well. For instance, I find that not being bogged down with technical details of a cognitively demanding but standardized steps like the C build process allows me to be more creative and inventive. Things can feel like they "just work." Is there a world where I would want to be that guy who can do all this down to bare metal? Sure, but it takes a lot of work to get there, and I'm not in a position to really do that right now.

And for a lot of people trying to use AI tools to transform productivity in their workplace, it seems like they are in a similar situation. There is a lot they could dive into in terms of the nuts and bolts, but because there aren't appreciable upward adjustments in the value of the output relative to the effort input, it simply isn't worth it.

But for a while now, I've intentionally taken the approach of being a "nuts and bolts" guy. I pride myself in understanding every single line of code that I've used in every professional projects as well as the majority of my side projects. Why? After reflecting on this for a while, the answer that rang most true for me was surprising. I think I need this level of understanding to do a lot of these projects at all.

Projects always have two cognitive dimensions- cognizing a problem and cognizing a solution. While AI can be very good in certain situations when it comes to cognizing a solution, I have found that it's generally much worse at cognizing problems. My ability to even identify problems as ones where programming may provide an adequate solution requires that I be familiar enough with the technical side in order to have that perspective.

But why not offload this too? Why not just give AI all the details about your work assignments, or even your life and let it optimize it? I have two objections to this, and I hope you do too. The first is pragmatic. At this point in time, I simply have seen neither persuasively conclusive data to indicate that AI is able to do this well. The second objection is moral. It is a value and priority for me to make meaningful and significant decisions in my life.

So I'm in a sort of middle ground. Compared to many of my peers, I'm much more nuts and bolts. I complete most of my Advent of Code challenges with no or minimal AI help. I have constructed and trained machine learning models tensor by tensor, layer by layer in PyTorch. I also have a workable understanding of matrix mathematics, vector calculus, and linear algebra. But compared to people who work with AI on the technical side, I am but a mere child. I could not explain the inner workings of the PyTorch modules that I use, or how exactly CUDA does its magic when I queue it up.

A few months ago, I was at a professional conference, and one impression I came away with is that it seems like of folks with a technical inclination, there are two kinds: Wizards and Dwarves.

Wizards are showy. They have sparkly magical fireworks and big, pointy hats. They give you visions and phantasms to your hearts delight. Their fireballs and lightning strikes cut through the darkness in bright flashes. They seem to be able to make the impossible possible. The people I saw as wizards are often building publicly. They're often on panels and talks. The dissemination and notoriety of their work (and themselves) is a big part of their value.

Dwarves are more solitary. They have tools that they know, love, and use. They focus on the objects of their efforts, not how those objects might be shared. It is a joy for them to improve and iterate over time. They see the sting of failure as integral to the thrill of success. For the dwarf, progress is a determined largely by a technical outcome, not social impact. As much as the role of the dwarf differs from the wizard, dwarves are also unique by their motivations, perspectives, and priorities.

The wizard will tell you how much their students love a new tool or demonstration. The dwarf will tell you how much time that tool saves on average with sample sizes and error bars. The wizard will tell you what emerging AI tool is most exciting. The dwarf will tell you how they tortured the new tool to find out how it probably works and how effective it is. The wizard will seek to establish themself as an outspoken expert among their peers. The dwarf will ask to be evaluated simply by the fruits of his labor.

I came away from that conference thinking "I am a dwarf."

And is there a place for a middle-ground dwarf in the current of AI democratization? I think so. When I was a kid, I played with actual Legos. Our Legos were hand-me downs, random pieces we got from friends at church or found at a community reuse center. As such, there were never any complete sets, let alone instructions. I was empowered to see the pieces as what they actually were, outside of the obligations or wider narrative of their productization. And my creations were fantastic. They could at times be just as large and complex as the largest Lego sets, but entirely malleable to my preferences. If I wanted to change something, I could because I understood what each brick in each place did. I remember having friends over to "play with Legos" and our play styles clashing. To them, to "play with Legos" was to construct stories about characters and interactions as if the Legos were figurines, sets, and props. Imagine their bewilderment when I upturned a bin of disorganized chaos at our feet and said to just start making. Wizard, meet dwarf.

What I concocted was different. Maybe not better in any sort of artistic sense, but for sure different, and certainly still of value. I think that's the avenue for dwarves these days. It's easy to let a single perspective drive discourse about emerging technologies. It's even easier to let those perspectives go unchallenged. Dwarves, as ones who ignore these narratival boxes, can also be the ones best equipped to break out of them.

I write this half as analysis of my professional and personal position in my field, as well as a sort of manifesto for me to embrace my dwarfishness. This path may not be that of flashy pageantry, but can lead to places that wizards cannot go.
