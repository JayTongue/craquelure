Today is my last day in the office for my current job. While I am moving on to a new position at a new organization that I am very excited about, this old job has been a very good place to me for the last four-ish years. It was the first "real" job I've had that wasn't temporary or under-employment relative to my qualifications. However, that's not saying much. Although I had either completed or was completing the required education for my role, I really didn't know anything about how to do this job when I first began.

I can remember one memory pretty clearly. When I first started, hours at the reference desk still went to 6:00 PM, and I'd be assigned a few of them every week. Since almost everyone went home at 5:00, these extra hours were often eerie, especially those days when the library was open but the law school wasn't. I was always afraid that during that hour, I'd get ambushed by a reference question I wouldn't be able to answer. One day, I had to get something from the basement during that witching hour. I had been shown the basement before, but hadn't needed anything from it yet. It's foreboding rolling compact shelving loomed over my mind as I was preparing to head down there. Luckily, there was another (more experienced) librarian in the office finishing some stuff up. When I ran into her on my way down, she dropped everything to head down with me. We found the book without too much trouble. In many ways, that's what being at my current position has been like. People were largely willing to be helpful and collegial.

These are some reflections on this position, role, and institution.

1. Promises and Actions, investments, growth.

If this job was so great, why am I leaving? This feels weird to say, but I think I outgrew it. This feels weird because there are plenty of smart, ambitious people who stay in jobs like mine their entire career. Maybe it's my millennial conditioning, but I used to scoff at people who would say that they switched jobs because they "wanted a change" or something. These sentiments felt frivolous, and I felt like I'd be lucky to even have one job to begin with, let alone one that I could discard to pursue another one.

But the truth is, to some extent I do feel like I've outgrown this role. A quick look around this blog will show that I'm very interested in tech, both in terms of large scale AI technologies, but also doing programming, soldering microcontrollers, and doing the manual and menial stuff. You may be shocked to know that none of this is actually part of the job that I have been hired for. Over the last week, I've had a few meetings with coworkers to explain an aspect of my role in order to pass those responsibilities to them. In one meeting, I was explaining how our website accessibility scanner works. It would do a scan, find something like insufficient font contrast, then you could use your browser inspector to locate that exact element on the page, then go into the custom CSS/HTML to change the font contrast. The issue, however, is that because of the low-code environment we use to host our website, not every class is one that we've explicitly declared. I was trying to explain this, and found myself drawing a diagram to give a brief over Object Oriented Programming more broadly, and how that paradigm informs classes and IDs in CSS. My very intelligent but non-technical coworker was able to follow along, but I could tell that thinking about systems this way was new to her.

My realization was that this was a bucket of knowledge and capability that I was bringing to this role that was outside of the actual requirements of the role. There have been a few instances where I've show the willingness and capacity to take on projects that involved more computing. These projects had data gathering, data analysis, custom pipelines, and other areas where a little bit of knowledge about statistical methods, programming, and web requests goes a long way. Not even the AI stuff. Just plain ol' deterministic programming the way God intended. I've been told by supervisors that I could do these projects, but not to advertise them, since that might make other professors want us to do these types of projects, and institutionally, that isn't what they wanted. That made it pretty clear to me that it wasn't just that they didn't reward me or incentivize me to provide value in these ways, they were also skeptical about the value of these contributions to begin with.

I had some talks with higher-ups at the institution at one point in time, where I was pushing for a role change that would augment my responsibilities and allow me to not only take on these projects, but to have a budget for things like conferences and hardware, as well as the ability to take a class every now and then to keep learning.

Some decision-makers were supportive of this. Some were not. I don't know exactly who was on what side. But the result is that the change never happened. Well, then I guess my option to stay and grow isn't very possible. I guess I'll go somewhere where it is.

2. The Friction

One thing I believe more firmly than ever, especially in the age of AI, is that you can't grow without learning, and you can't learn without friction. At the end of this week, I'm presenting at a professional conference. I didn't want to use a boring powerpoint like everyone else probably will be. Since I'm presenting on a somewhat technical topic, I tried my hand at learning Manim, a fantastic open-source Python animation library. I'm really excited with my presentation materials, and the fact that I can do things like create multiple visual elements with a list comprehension instead of drag-and-drop alone was worth it.

But at first, I was very bad at it. I know Python, but this library uses custom classes, methods, and a non-standard compiler. (This may not technically be compiling, but your Python renders into movie files and HTML code.) I struggled with being bad a lot. I read docs a lot. I asked AI a lot of questions. But I steadily got better. Since I slowed down and went through that process, I can look at my project and understand every single line of code in there. I can also sit down with just and editor and bang out the majority of the code I need because of the experience I now have struggling through the syntax and learning the methods.

I could feel the benefits of this learning later on. Although I wasn't typing any faster, my ability to use my understanding of the library's tools and workflow mapped onto my desire visual outcome much faster. I was able to mentally break down desired outcomes into a series of steps and "see" how I could use the tools I was increasingly familiar with to get there.

I've been ruminating on a quote from John Ousterhout recently: "A little bit of slope makes up for a lot of y-intercept."

Four years ago, I had zero knowledge of Python. I sat down and went through the friction of "Hello World" and off-by-one errors. Today, I can figure out most Advent of Code problems with just docs. Three years ago, I had zero knowledge of web services. I went through the friction of trying to learn Django, and became the website manager for my org, and the host of this Flask site. Two months ago, I had zero knowledge of Manim. This week, I'll be doing a presentation with it at a professional conference. There are so many instances where my intercept was zero, and friction was how I gained my slope.

This is especially poignant in today's world. Apparently today's youths(?) think that struggling with anything is cringe and doing too much. Everyone wants to already be good. No one wants the struggle to get there. This seems to manifest in everything from learning how to do a kickflip to painting. People only want the impenetrable facade of accomplishment, not the soft white underbelly of the failures it took to get there.

This is compounded by the idea of corporate efficiency and AI. I could have vibe coded my Manim presentation by just describing what I wanted to the AI without learning what the code it spat out meant, and that would have been a lot easier on my brain. It might have been faster too, at least at first. But no, I embraced the friction.

3. Proximity to Death

This last reflection is a bit of a pivot from the first two, but in some ways, is far more important.

The first class I taught by myself, one of my students contacted me to let me know that their mentor had passed away recently in a plane crash. This past semester, a student of mine let me know that a sibling of his partner had also passed away. Each time, I've been personally taken aback. I've long felt like our 21st century American society lacks the ability to contend with death, grief, illness, and suffering in human and healthy ways. In both instances, I've extended personal condolences, approved any absences, and informed them about resources for mental health and student wellness available through the school.

But it's humbling to think of how close death constantly is. A car accident out of nowhere. A faulty garage door spring. A blood vessel in your brain that decides not to do its job.

I don't think I was a brilliant or amazing instructor at all. I definitely tried, but I often felt pretty certain that what I said went in one ear and out the other, if not that class session, then at least at the end of the semester. Instead of being a great instructor, I hope that I'd be remembered as an instructor who cared. I hope that if they remember be, it would be for my ability to value their presence and dignity as humans for that short window of time that I was with them.
